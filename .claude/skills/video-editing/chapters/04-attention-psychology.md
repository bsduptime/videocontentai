# Chapter 4: Attention Psychology

The science behind every editing decision. This chapter provides actionable rules derived
from eye-tracking, neuroscience, and cognitive psychology research.

---

## 4.1 Eye Tracking: Where Viewers Look

- **Faces dominate gaze.** When a face is on screen, viewers look at it. Return to the
  speaker's face within 5-8 seconds of any B-roll cutaway.
- **Center bias.** Viewers default to screen center. Place primary subject or text at center
  or rule-of-thirds intersections. Never place critical info in corners without an explicit
  visual cue (arrow, zoom) directing attention there.
- **Blink rate tracks engagement.** Blinks cluster at narrative boundaries — the brain uses
  them as thought punctuation. Place cuts at natural thought boundaries (topic shifts,
  sentence endings, pauses). This aligns with Murch's blink theory.
- **Peripheral motion captures involuntarily.** The dorsal visual pathway is 90%
  direction-sensitive. Looming/expanding motion captures attention within 200ms.
- **62% of YouTube mobile viewing receives active visual attention** vs 45% for TV.
  Mobile viewers are more focused but for shorter bursts.

## 4.2 Gestalt Principles for Editing

| Principle | Rule for Editing |
|-----------|-----------------|
| **Proximity** | Shots close together in time are perceived as related. Direct cuts (<0.5s) = same scene. Long dissolves (>1s) = scene/time break. |
| **Similarity** | Match color temperature, brightness, framing between consecutive shots in same scene. Mismatched grades signal a topic change. |
| **Continuity** | If subject moves left-to-right in shot A, next shot preserves that flow. Breaking this creates disorientation (useful for pattern interrupts). |
| **Closure** | Brain fills gaps. Show beginning and result — viewer completes the middle. Enables jump cuts in tutorials without confusion. |
| **Common fate** | Elements moving together are grouped. Animate text in same direction as camera motion. Text sliding left while camera pans right = cognitive conflict. |

## 4.3 The Von Restorff Isolation Effect

Making one moment visually distinctive makes it memorable. The more homogeneous the
surrounding context, the stronger the isolation.

### Rules

- Before a key moment, establish a visual pattern for 10-15 seconds (consistent framing,
  color, pace). Then break it sharply.
- For the most important moment, change **at least TWO** visual dimensions simultaneously:
  color grade shift + zoom, or text size + font color.
- Reserve truly distinctive treatments for 1-2 moments per segment. Overuse destroys the
  effect.
- The effect is stronger with passive viewing (social media scrollers).

### Practical Formula

For a 10-minute video, identify 3-4 peak moments. Give each a unique visual treatment not
used anywhere else: a specific color flash, a unique transition, a text style. These become
the moments viewers remember and share.

## 4.4 Edit Blindness and Change Blindness

25-33% of continuity cuts go completely unnoticed — "edit blindness."

### Rules

- **Cut during motion, not stillness.** Match-action cuts are nearly invisible.
- **Discontinuity cuts require LESS cognitive effort** than continuity cuts. When changing
  topic, use a clear discontinuity (different background, color shift, graphic bumper).
- **Audio continuity bridges visual cuts.** Continuous audio (voice, music) across a visual
  cut makes it feel smooth. Break both audio AND visual for deliberate pattern interrupts.
- **Attention narrows at cut points.** Place the most important element of each new shot
  near center-frame for the first 0.5-1 second.
- **You can change more between cuts than you think** — background, positioning, wardrobe —
  as long as the primary subject and their emotional state remain consistent.

## 4.5 Cognitive Load (Mayer's Principles)

| Principle | Rule |
|-----------|------|
| **Dual-channel** | Never display text that duplicates what is being spoken. Either show text OR speak — not both saying the same thing. |
| **Signaling** | When showing complex visuals (code, dashboard), highlight the relevant portion. Dim or blur the rest. |
| **Segmenting** | Present one concept per segment. Insert 0.5-1s visual separator between chunks. |
| **Coherence** | Remove everything that doesn't serve the objective. No decorative animations during complex explanations. |
| **Temporal contiguity** | Visual must appear within 1-2 seconds of narration describing it. |
| **Max elements** | 3-4 simultaneous on-screen elements (subject, one text, one graphic). Beyond this, comprehension degrades. |

## 4.6 Motion and Attention Direction

- **Looming (expanding) motion captures attention.** Use slow zoom-in to build tension and
  pull focus. Zoom-out to release tension and signal transition.
- **Expanding motion directs attention to the focus of expansion.** When zooming into a
  frame portion, that point becomes the involuntary focus.
- **Peripheral motion is distracting when unintentional.** Animated elements should appear
  and settle within 0.5s, or not appear during critical narrative moments.
- **Left-to-right = forward progress.** Animate text entries left-to-right for momentum.
  Right-to-left for contrast or conflict.
- **Pan speed**: Slow enough for eye tracking (~5-10 degrees/sec). Faster requires
  motivation (action sequence, energy burst).

## 4.7 Color Psychology

| Desired Mood | Temperature | Saturation | Contrast | Application |
|-------------|-------------|-----------|----------|-------------|
| Tension/Drama | Cool (blue) | Moderate-High | High | Deep blacks, sharp whites |
| Comfort | Warm (orange) | Moderate-High | Low | Lifted blacks, soft rolloff |
| Documentary/Truth | Neutral | Moderate | Balanced | Credibility signaling |
| Energy/Excess | Warm or Cool | High | High | Stimulating but risks fatigue |
| Nostalgia | Warm | Low-Moderate | Low | Faded, aged look |

### Specific Rules

- **Red** increases adrenaline and attention → urgency, CTAs, hook moments
- **Blue** promotes rational thinking → explanation segments, tutorials
- **Desaturation** focuses attention on subject → reduce saturation 20-40% for concentration
- **High contrast = tension, low contrast = calm** → modulate within a video for emotional pacing
- **Color grade shifts signal segment changes** → subtle temperature shift on topic change

## 4.8 Pattern Interrupt Frequency

The brain's Reticular Activating System (RAS) is a "novelty filter" that evolved to detect
environmental changes.

### Frequency by Audience

| Audience | Visual change interval | Burst sequence |
|----------|----------------------|----------------|
| Young (13-24) | Every 15-25 seconds | Every 2-3 minutes |
| General (25+) | Every 25-40 seconds | Every 3-4 minutes |

### Frequency by Video Phase

| Phase | Minutes | Change interval |
|-------|---------|----------------|
| Opening | 0-3 | Every 10-20 seconds |
| Hooked | 3-7 | Every 25-40 seconds |
| After minute 8 | 8+ | Calm 30-40s + burst (5-10 rapid cuts in 10-15s) |

## 4.9 Attention Span: Reality vs. Myth

The "8-second attention span" is a myth (debunked — original data was fabricated). What IS real:

- **Attention on a single screen averages 47 seconds** (down from 2.5 minutes in 2004).
  Deliver payoff or visual change within every 45 seconds.
- **The first 8 seconds are a decision point**, not a limit. Viewers decide whether to
  continue within ~8 seconds. No logos, no "hey guys", no preamble.
- **50-60% of Shorts viewers drop in first 3 seconds.** First frame must be visually arresting.
- **55% of long-form viewers drop within 60 seconds.** Front-load engaging content.
- **Short-form consumption correlates with reduced sustained attention** (r = -0.38).
  Design for impaired attention of heavy social media users.

## 4.10 The Peak-End Rule

People judge an experience by its most intense moment (peak) and its final moment (end),
NOT by the average.

### Rules

- Structure every video with at least one high-intensity peak + a strong ending.
- The middle can be lower-energy if peak and end are strong.
- Do not repeat the same emotional beat — each peak should be different (surprise, then
  humor, then awe, then tension release).
- Duration neglect: a 5-minute video with one great peak and strong ending is remembered
  more favorably than 5 minutes of "pretty good."

### Peak Placement Guide

| Peak | Position | Purpose |
|------|----------|---------|
| Opening peak | 0-8 seconds | Gets them to stay |
| First content peak | 60-90 seconds | Confirms their choice to watch |
| Mid-video peak | Halfway point | Re-engagement |
| Climax peak | 80-90% through | The payoff |
| End | Final 10 seconds | What they remember (peak-end rule) |
